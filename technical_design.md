# Build4Good 2026 — Technical Design Document

---

## Data + Fidelity

**What exact chlorophyll dataset did you use?**
We attempted to use NASA NEO (Natural Earth Observations) MODIS chlorophyll-a product (MODISA_L3m_CHL) at 0.1°/pixel resolution (~9km at equator), covering the North Pacific (130°E–215°E, 20°N–55°N). During development we discovered the cached file (`chla_neo_cache.png`) was actually an NDVI (vegetation index) composite, not chlorophyll. The final deployed texture uses a **synthetic chlorophyll field** generated to match known North Pacific oceanographic structure (Kuroshio Extension, North Pacific Gyre, subarctic front), informed by the real geographic data but not directly pixel-mapped from it.

**What preprocessing steps were applied to preserve filament structures?**
- Log compression: `log(1 + chla)` to prevent high-concentration coastal blooms from blowing out
- Gaussian smoothing at multiple spatial scales to suppress pixel-level noise
- Land masking via pixel color detection from the source image (`r > b + 0.06` identifies land)
- Mesoscale eddy injection (24 synthetic eddies placed at oceanographically plausible locations)
- Curl-noise turbulence field layered over the base current to generate divergence-free fine-scale filaments
- LIC (Line Integral Convolution) at 4096×2048 with 25 bidirectional steps on white noise — this is what produces the visible streamlines

**How did you handle missing data (cloud cover, gaps)?**
Cloud cover and data gaps were not handled from real data — we shifted to a fully synthetic field early in development. The land mask is applied as a hard cutoff; ocean regions with structurally missing data are filled by the synthetic field. This is the biggest honest gap between the visualization and real science.

**What is the final grid resolution compared to the original NASA data?**
- Original NASA MODIS: ~9km/pixel at 0.1° resolution
- Generated LIC texture: 4096×2048 pixels computed, downsampled to 2048×1024 for the final PNG
- World space: 80×40 Unity world units, displayed across the screen
- The texture resolution significantly exceeds what the underlying data supports — fine filament structures at sub-pixel level are generated by LIC, not resolved from real data

**Are we interpolating between cells or generating new structure entirely?**
The LIC algorithm generates new structure. It integrates a noise field along the flow vector field, creating coherent streamlines that did not exist in the original data. The flow field itself is synthetic (curl-noise + eddies). So we are generating visually plausible structure consistent with the physics, not interpolating real measurements.

## Currents + Physics

**What current dataset did you use?**
No external current dataset (e.g., OSCAR) was loaded at runtime. The flow field is synthetic: a primary Kuroshio Extension vector `(0.85, 0.22)` normalized (roughly eastward with slight northward component), layered with curl-noise at two spatial frequencies and 24 mesoscale eddies with Gaussian influence radii.

**Are we using raw u/v vectors or derived flow directions?**
Derived/approximated. The primary current vector is hardcoded from known Kuroshio Extension direction. Eddies add rotational perturbations. No raw u/v NetCDF data was parsed at runtime.

**How are currents mapped onto the grid?**
Curl-noise ensures the field is divergence-free (no artificial sources/sinks). Each eddy contributes a rotation field weighted by a Gaussian falloff. The LIC integration walks along this field to produce the streaklines.

**How stable is the flow field over time?**
The background LIC texture is static (pre-baked PNG). The HLSL shader applies a time-varying UV distortion using multi-frequency sine waves to simulate shear and eddy motion visually. This is not physically time-stepped — it is a visual approximation of a time-varying field.

## Simulation Model

**What is the exact advection rule being used?**
For the pre-baked LIC: Euler integration along the flow vector field, 25 steps bidirectional, step size ~0.5 pixels. For the shader animation: UV coordinates are displaced by `sin(uv.y * f + t * speed) * amplitude` at two frequencies, approximating shear flow. For wake particles: explicit position update via Unity physics with initial velocity set to `trailDirection * speed + current + noise`.

**How is decay implemented?**
Particle alpha fades linearly over lifetime (2.5–4.5 seconds). LIC intensity in the pre-baked texture is fixed; decay is purely visual (alpha over lifetime curve).

**How is lateral diffusion modeled?**
Via `Random.insideUnitCircle * noiseScale` added to each particle's initial velocity. In the LIC computation, the white noise seed provides inherent lateral spread — adjacent streamlines diverge naturally.

**What nonlinear function is used to suppress low values?**
Log compression `log(1 + x)` during texture generation. Low-intensity ocean regions (clear water) compress toward dark blue; high-intensity regions (blooms, filaments) compress toward white, preserving dynamic range without blowout.

**How is "directional amplification" implemented?**
LIC naturally amplifies structure along the flow direction — noise components perpendicular to flow average out over integration steps, while components parallel to flow reinforce. This produces the visible elongated filaments without explicit amplification code.

## Visual Design

**How is intensity mapped to color?**
Grayscale LIC streamline value → blended between deep navy `(0.008, 0.022, 0.055)` and bright white via the pre-baked PNG. Land regions are masked to olive-green. The HLSL shader samples this texture at a distorted UV coordinate, preserving the relative intensity mapping.

**What thresholds determine when glow appears?**
No explicit glow pass. Bright filaments emerge naturally where LIC integration produces high coherence (aligned flow). The shader's UV distortion can briefly align multiple bright regions, producing momentary intensity peaks that read as glow.

**How did you avoid the "blown-out" look?**
Log compression during texture generation. Eddy spiral arms were removed (they created `cos(arm_angle * 2.5)` spikes). The final texture has ~69% of pixels below 0.1 intensity — mostly dark ocean with sparse bright filaments.

**How are particles spawned and updated relative to the grid?**
Wake particles are spawned in Unity world space at the boat's stern using `ParticleSystem.Emit()` with explicit world-space position and velocity. They are not tied to the texture grid — they exist in the physics simulation. The Kuroshio current direction `(0.85, 0.22)` is shared between the texture flow and particle drift so they appear consistent.

**Are particles tied to intensity, flow, or both?**
Flow only. Particle count scales with boat speed; direction uses the actual `Rigidbody2D.linearVelocity` for the trail direction plus a gentle ocean current drift.

## Performance / Engineering

**How large is the full world grid?**
80×40 Unity world units. The LIC texture is 2048×1024 pixels. The ocean is rendered as a single quad mesh that follows the camera.

**How often is the simulation updated?**
The HLSL shader runs every frame (GPU). Wake particles are emitted every 0.18 seconds. The background texture is static — no per-frame CPU simulation.

**What rendering approach is used?**
Single fullscreen quad with a custom URP HLSL unlit shader (`Build4Good/ScrollingWater`). The shader applies multi-frequency UV distortion in the fragment shader using `_Time.y`, sampling the pre-baked LIC PNG. No render textures, compute shaders, or tile streaming.

**What were the biggest performance bottlenecks?**
LIC generation in Python (offline, ~30–90s per image at 4096×2048). At runtime, no measurable bottleneck — single quad draw call, 400 max particles, no physics other than one Rigidbody2D.

**How did you ensure smooth panning across the world?**
The ocean quad follows `Camera.main` position each frame. UV offset is `camPosition / (oceanSize * 2)` — half-scale parallax using `TextureWrapMode.Mirror` to avoid hard seams at texture boundaries.

## Accuracy vs Interpretation

**Which parts are strictly data-driven?**
- Geographic extent (North Pacific, Kuroshio Extension latitude/longitude region)
- Approximate current direction vector (from published Kuroshio climatology)
- Land mask shape (derived from the NASA cached image)

**Which parts are modeled or inferred?**
- All chlorophyll values (synthetic)
- All filament structures (LIC on white noise)
- Eddy positions and sizes (plausible but not from real observations)
- Temporal animation (pure visual approximation)

**How far does the simulation deviate from real ocean physics?**
Significantly. The LIC texture is a static artistic interpretation, not a numerical ocean model. No thermodynamics, no salinity, no seasonal variation, no tide forcing, no baroclinic instability. The eddies are idealized Gaussian vortices, not data-assimilated.

**What assumptions were made to fill the gaps?**
- Kuroshio is always eastward at constant speed
- Mesoscale eddies are stationary, symmetric, and evenly distributed
- No cloud cover or sensor gaps need filling (synthetic field)
- The visual filament structure is scale-invariant

**If we removed the simulation layer, what would the visualization lose?**
The entire fine-scale filament structure — without LIC, you would see only the smooth background color gradient and eddy circles. The "strings" that make the ocean look real are entirely generated by the simulation.

## UX / Interaction

**How is boat movement implemented?**
The boat is a `Rigidbody2D` with `linearVelocity = -transform.up * moveSpeed` set every `FixedUpdate`. The camera follows via `Vector3.Lerp` in `LateUpdate`. The ocean quad follows the camera in `Update`. The boat physically moves through world space; the camera and ocean follow.

**Is movement tied to the data?**
Independent. The Kuroshio direction `(0.85, 0.22)` is shared as a constant between the particle wake system and the texture flow direction for visual consistency, but the boat is not advected by the current — it moves at constant speed regardless of flow.

**What parameters are user-adjustable?**
- `moveSpeed` — boat forward speed (BoatController)
- `turnSpeed`, `turnInertia`, `turnDecay` — steering feel
- `sternOffset` — wake spawn position relative to pivot
- `particleDirection` — trail direction multiplier
- Camera `orthographicSize` — zoom level
- Shader distortion amplitudes and speeds (hardcoded but easily exposed)

**What debugging views exist?**
None at runtime. During development: Python preview scripts generated `v2_1_subtle.png` through `v2_5_max.png` showing LIC variants; `chl_preview_seed.png` showed the raw chlorophyll seed field.

## Framing / Narrative

**What is the single most important insight this system reveals?**
The ocean is not a uniform body of water — it has texture, structure, and direction. The Kuroshio Extension is one of the most energetic current systems on Earth, transporting heat from the tropics toward the poles, and its filaments and eddies structure marine ecosystems at scales visible from space.

**What would a scientist critique about this model?**
That it shows none of the real data. The chlorophyll is synthetic, the eddies are idealized, the current is a single constant vector, and the animation is a shader trick. A real oceanographer would want real HYCOM or ROMS output, real MODIS chlorophyll Level-3 products, and time-varying fields with proper eddy tracking.

**What would a non-technical viewer learn in 5 seconds?**
The ocean has currents — you can see them as moving lines — and sailing with or against them would feel different.

**What design choice had the biggest impact on visual quality?**
LIC on white noise (rather than on the chlorophyll field directly). Seeding LIC from white noise produces clean, continuous, hair-like filaments. Seeding from the blobby chlorophyll field produced thick, artifact-ridden smears. The white noise seed, combined with the synthetic flow field, is what makes the ocean look like the satellite imagery it's meant to evoke.
